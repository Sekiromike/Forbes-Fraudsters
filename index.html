The Situation (It's Bad)

Since 2011, Forbes has been running a prestigious annual list to identify the next generation of industry titans. Somewhere along the way it also became a remarkably efficient funnel into federal court. We're not saying it's a scam detector — but we're not not saying that either.

The Part That Should Bother You: Forbes 30 Under 30 alumni raised ~$5.3B in legitimate venture capital. They were then arrested for or implicated in frauds totalling over $63.4B. That's a 3.5× fraud-to-funding ratio — meaning for every dollar a Forbes-blessed founder legitimately raised, they were accused of stealing three-fifty. A magazine. Did this. So. Why. Though. 🧠

Six reasons why the smartest financial institutions on earth kept giving billions of dollars to people who were, in some cases, literally running Excel-powered confetti machines and calling it a unicorn. (We are not better than this. We are going to do it again.)

Forbes' Official Defense: The magazine cited a 99.9% "success rate" across 10,000+ honorees. The 0.1% in question? Responsible for $63+ billion in fraud — more than the GDP of several nations. By that logic, the Titanic had a 99.99% deck-survival rate. Most of the deck was fine! The deck was great. We're focusing on the wrong thing.
01

Free Money Brain Rot (The ZIRP Era) 🧻

From 2012 to 2022, interest rates were basically zero, which meant institutional money had nowhere to go except into the hands of charismatic twentysomethings with decks full of graphs going up and to the right. Due diligence was a vibe. Charlie Javice made a fake list of 4 million students on a $18,000 bounty from a data professor. JP Morgan Chase — the largest bank in America — bought it for $175 million. We have nothing more to add.

02

"Fake It Till You Make It" Was Not a Joke 🎭

Silicon Valley told a generation of founders: pretend to be bigger than you are and the money will make it real. This is fine advice until "bigger than you are" means "four million users you invented" or "$100M ARR that is actually $15M and we are going to prison." The market rewarded scale metrics over revenue metrics, so the logical move was to fabricate scale metrics. We did this. We as a society. We are the problem.

03

The Magazine Said It Was Fine 📰

Forbes put them on a list. VCs used the list as a substitute for doing their jobs. Forbes used VC backing as a criterion for the list. Nobody audited anything. JP Morgan acquired a company for $175M based on a spreadsheet of fake names. SoftBank gave $170M to a social app without verifying it had any real users. This is due diligence in the modern era: Google the founder, see Forbes cover, wire the money. We built a whole economy on this.

04

Staying Private Indefinitely Is a Feature 🛡️

Public companies must file audited financials with the SEC. Private companies must file nothing except their vibes and a pitch deck. Rishi Shah built a $5.6B pharma advertising empire on screens that didn't exist — and operated for years before anyone checked. In a previous era, going public would have required showing an auditor the screens. The screens were not there. Nobody checked for the screens.

05

Black Turtleneck + Drop-out Backstory = Genius 🖤

Bill Gates dropped out of Harvard. Steve Jobs wore a turtleneck. Mark Zuckerberg was rude at meetings. Therefore: any young founder who is rude, dropped out, and wears minimalist clothing must be the next billionaire. This psychological shortcut — which Forbes literally monetizes as a franchise — let people like Bankman-Fried and Shkreli be erratic, dishonest, and openly contemptuous of rules and get more funding each time. "That's just how geniuses are." It was not.

06

It Went Global and Got Worse 🌍

Forbes helpfully expanded to Africa, Asia, and Europe, thoughtfully exporting the entire adverse selection mechanism to new continents. Obinwanne Okeke used Forbes Africa recognition as cover for an $11M cybercrime syndicate and wrote entrepreneurship columns while running it. Gökçe Güven made the 2025 list and was indicted almost immediately. The pipeline is not a quirk of American capitalism. It is structural. It is a feature not a bug. We're so tired.

Hot Take (Lukewarm, Actually. Room Temperature.): The era of free money is over, rates are up, and VCs are theoretically doing due diligence again. But the structural incentives that made this happen — a media franchise that profits from celebrating youth and disruption, a VC ecosystem that uses press coverage as a proxy for auditing, and a culture that mistakes arrogance for genius — are all still completely intact. The pipeline will continue. We will update this dashboard accordingly. We are not hopeful.
